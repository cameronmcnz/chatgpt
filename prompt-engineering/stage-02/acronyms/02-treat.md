# TREAT

TREAT is a simple way to make your prompts less vague and much more useful. Instead of tossing a request at ChatGPT and hoping for the best, you give it five things: the **Task**, the **Role**, **Examples**, the **Audience**, and the **Tone**.

Think of it like giving directions to a human coworker. If you only say, "Write something about email," you will get... something. If you use TREAT, you get something you can actually use without needing a rescue mission afterward.

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| T, Task | Task | States exactly what you want ChatGPT to do |
| R, Role | Role | Tells ChatGPT what perspective or identity to use |
| E, Examples | Examples | Shows what good output looks like |
| A, Audience | Audience | Defines who the answer is for |
| T, Tone | Tone | Sets the style, mood, or voice of the response |

## Why this template works

TREAT belongs in **Controlled Prompting**, which means you are not just asking for an answer, you are shaping the answer.

Here is why it works so well:

- **Task** removes ambiguity. ChatGPT knows the job.
- **Role** changes the lens. A teacher, marketer, analyst, and coach will answer differently.
- **Examples** reduce guesswork. Showing a pattern is often stronger than describing one.
- **Audience** keeps the response at the right level. No jargon avalanche unless you actually want one.
- **Tone** makes the result sound appropriate. Formal, friendly, persuasive, calm, punchy, and so on.

Without these ingredients, prompts often come back generic. With them, answers become more consistent, more reusable, and less likely to sound like a robot trying to blend in at a staff meeting.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> TASK: Write a 150-word welcome email for new employees on their first day.  
> ROLE: Act as a professional HR manager.  
> EXAMPLES: Use a style similar to, "We're excited to welcome you to the team. Your first week will focus on orientation, training, and meeting key colleagues."  
> AUDIENCE: New office employees at a mid-sized company.  
> TONE: Warm, professional, and reassuring.

**How the template shows up:**
- **Task:** "Write a 150-word welcome email for new employees on their first day" defines the exact assignment.
- **Role:** "Act as a professional HR manager" gives ChatGPT the right workplace perspective.
- **Examples:** The sample sentence shows the kind of wording and content expected.
- **Audience:** "New office employees at a mid-sized company" tells ChatGPT who will read it.
- **Tone:** "Warm, professional, and reassuring" shapes how the email should sound.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt is clear, controlled, and easy for ChatGPT to follow. The task sets the goal, the role creates credibility, the example gives a style reference, the audience keeps the content relevant, and the tone prevents the email from sounding stiff or weirdly cheerful, like a training video from 1994.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> Help me create a short LinkedIn post announcing a free beginner workshop on budgeting.  
>  
> - Write as a financial coach  
> - Use this kind of line as inspiration: "Good money habits start with simple steps"  
> - Make it for adults who feel intimidated by budgeting  
> - Keep the tone encouraging and practical

**How the template shows up:**
- **Task:** "Help me create a short LinkedIn post announcing a free beginner workshop on budgeting" explains the job.
- **Role:** "Write as a financial coach" sets the perspective.
- **Examples:** "Good money habits start with simple steps" gives a sample line to imitate loosely.
- **Audience:** "Adults who feel intimidated by budgeting" identifies the reader.
- **Tone:** "Encouraging and practical" tells ChatGPT how the post should feel.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version feels more natural than a rigid form, but it still gives strong control. The example prevents the post from sounding random, the audience keeps the message accessible, and the tone helps avoid sounding preachy. That matters because nobody wants budgeting advice that sounds like a scolding from a disappointed spreadsheet.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> Write a short product description for a reusable water bottle as if you're a smart retail copywriter. Use wording similar to "simple, durable, and made for everyday life." Make it for busy parents who want less plastic waste, and keep it upbeat and clear.

**How the template shows up:**
- **Task:** "Write a short product description for a reusable water bottle" gives the assignment.
- **Role:** "As if you're a smart retail copywriter" supplies the role.
- **Examples:** "Use wording similar to 'simple, durable, and made for everyday life'" provides an example of style.
- **Audience:** "Busy parents who want less plastic waste" defines who the description is aimed at.
- **Tone:** "Keep it upbeat and clear" sets the voice.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt does not label each field, but TREAT is still there underneath. That makes it feel natural while staying precise. The result is more likely to sound like useful product copy instead of a bland list of features. In other words, it helps ChatGPT sound more like a competent writer and less like a label printer with opinions.

## Warnings and extra tips

TREAT is powerful, but it is not magic. A few things to watch for:

- **Do not make the task fuzzy.** "Write something good" is not a task. Be specific.
- **Do not overload the role.** "Act as a lawyer, comedian, scientist, and pirate" is entertaining, but not always helpful.
- **Make examples short and relevant.** One or two examples are usually enough. Too many can make the model copy too closely.
- **Audience matters more than people think.** A response for executives should not sound like a response for fifth graders, and vice versa.
- **Tone should be intentional.** If you skip tone, ChatGPT will guess. Sometimes it guesses well. Sometimes it guesses like a karaoke machine trying jazz.
- **TREAT does not cover everything.** If you also need formatting rules, word limits, guardrails, or things to avoid, add them. This template helps control content and style, but some tasks need extra boundaries.
- **Examples are guidance, not handcuffs.** Use them to steer the output, not to force an exact imitation.

A good practical habit is this: if a result feels off, check which part of TREAT is missing. Usually it is one of these:
- the task was too broad,
- the audience was unclear,
- the tone was never specified,
- or there was no example to anchor the style.

## Quick summary

TREAT helps you write better prompts by giving ChatGPT five clear signals: **Task, Role, Examples, Audience, Tone**. That means less guessing, better output, and fewer disappointing responses that technically answer the question while somehow missing the point.

To practice, pick one real task you already do, such as writing an email, a social post, or a product description, and rewrite your prompt using all five parts of TREAT. Try it once with labels, then again in a more natural sentence. You will quickly see how much better the response gets when you stop hoping and start steering.
