# POWER

If your prompts feel a little too "good luck, machine, figure it out," use **POWER**.

POWER is a simple way to make prompts more controlled, more useful, and a lot less random. It gives ChatGPT five things it loves: a role to play, a clear goal, safety boundaries, examples to follow, and a specific kind of output to produce. In other words, you stop tossing vague requests into the void and start directing the response like someone who has seen a meeting agenda before.

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **P, Persona** | **Role** | Tells ChatGPT what identity or perspective to use |
| **O, Objective** | **Goal** | States exactly what you want the model to accomplish |
| **W, Warnings** | **Guardrails** | Says what to avoid, be careful about, or not include |
| **E, Examples** | **Examples** | Shows what good output looks like |
| **R, Result** | **Result** | Defines the final format, style, or deliverable |

## Why this template works

POWER belongs to **Controlled Prompting**, which means you are not just asking for an answer, you are shaping it.

A basic prompt might say, "Write me a summary." That can work, sort of. But the result may be too long, too vague, too formal, too casual, or just oddly confident about things it made up five seconds ago.

POWER fixes that by adding structure:

- **Persona** improves voice and perspective.
- **Objective** removes guesswork about the task.
- **Warnings** prevent common mistakes and unwanted content.
- **Examples** give the model a pattern to copy.
- **Result** tells it what the finished answer should look like.

The big benefit is reliability. You can reuse a POWER prompt, tweak one part, and get more consistent results. That is useful for emails, marketing, studying, planning, writing, and basically any task where "surprise me" is not the best business strategy.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> **PERSONA:** You are a professional customer support writer for a software company.  
> **OBJECTIVE:** Write a clear apology email to a customer whose subscription was charged twice.  
> **WARNINGS:** Do not blame the customer. Do not use legal language. Do not promise a refund timeline unless it is confirmed.  
> **EXAMPLES:** Use wording similar in tone to this: "I’m sorry for the frustration this caused. I understand how annoying billing issues can be, and I appreciate your patience while we fix it."  
> **RESULT:** Produce a polished email of 120 to 150 words with a subject line and a friendly, professional tone.

**How the template shows up:**
- **P, Persona:** "Professional customer support writer for a software company."
- **O, Objective:** "Write a clear apology email to a customer whose subscription was charged twice."
- **W, Warnings:** "Do not blame the customer. Do not use legal language. Do not promise a refund timeline unless it is confirmed."
- **E, Examples:** The sample sentence gives the model a tone to imitate.
- **R, Result:** "A polished email of 120 to 150 words with a subject line and a friendly, professional tone."

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version removes a lot of ambiguity. Without POWER, the model might write something too stiff, too casual, or too risky. The role sets the voice, the objective defines the task, the warnings reduce damage, the example sets the tone, and the result field locks in the format. It is the difference between "send an email" and "send the email your manager will not rewrite in red ink."

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> Act like a high school science tutor.  
> Help me explain photosynthesis to a 13-year-old who gets bored easily.  
> Avoid jargon, and do not assume they already know biology terms.  
> Use this kind of style: short explanations, one simple example, then a quick recap question.  
> Give me the answer as:  
> - a 3 sentence explanation  
> - one everyday example  
> - one quiz question with the answer underneath

**How the template shows up:**
- **P, Persona:** "Act like a high school science tutor."
- **O, Objective:** "Help me explain photosynthesis to a 13-year-old who gets bored easily."
- **W, Warnings:** "Avoid jargon, and do not assume they already know biology terms."
- **E, Examples:** "Use this kind of style: short explanations, one simple example, then a quick recap question."
- **R, Result:** The bullet list defines the exact output structure.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt is less formal, but still very controlled. The tutor role shapes the explanation style. The objective focuses on teaching, not just defining. The warnings prevent the classic "simple explanation" that somehow still sounds like a textbook written by a committee. The example gives a teaching pattern, and the result turns the answer into something ready to use. Nice, practical, no lab coat required.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> Pretend you are a fitness coach who is realistic and encouraging. I want a beginner walking plan for someone who has been inactive for a while and gets overwhelmed easily. Please do not make it intense, do not include gym workouts, and do not use guilt-based motivation. Something like "start small, build consistency, and keep it simple" is the tone I want. Give me a 7-day plan with one short tip for each day.

**How the template shows up:**
- **P, Persona:** "Pretend you are a fitness coach who is realistic and encouraging."
- **O, Objective:** "I want a beginner walking plan for someone who has been inactive for a while and gets overwhelmed easily."
- **W, Warnings:** "Do not make it intense, do not include gym workouts, and do not use guilt-based motivation."
- **E, Examples:** The phrase "start small, build consistency, and keep it simple" acts as a style example.
- **R, Result:** "Give me a 7-day plan with one short tip for each day."

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This one sounds natural, like something a real person would actually type. But under the hood, it still uses POWER. That is the beauty of it. You do not have to sound robotic to be specific. The prompt gives the model enough direction to create a plan that is supportive, realistic, and usable, instead of some heroic "wake up at 4:30 a.m. and become a new species" nonsense.

## Warnings and extra tips

POWER is strong, but it is not magic. A few things to watch for:

- **Do not make the objective vague.** "Help with marketing" is fuzzy. "Write three email subject lines for a spring sale" is better.
- **Do not skip warnings when accuracy matters.** If the topic involves health, money, law, or anything risky, guardrails matter a lot.
- **Do not overload the prompt with too many rules.** If you pile on twenty instructions, the model may miss some. Prioritize the most important ones.
- **Examples help, but bad examples teach bad habits.** If your example is sloppy, the output may be too.
- **Result should be specific.** Say whether you want a list, paragraph, table, outline, script, or email.
- **POWER is not always enough by itself.** For complex tasks, you may also need audience, tone, context, source material, or step-by-step instructions.
- **Check the output.** Even a well-structured prompt can still produce errors. AI is helpful, not magical. It is more like an eager intern with excellent formatting skills.

## Quick summary

**POWER** stands for **Persona, Objective, Warnings, Examples, Result**. It helps you control responses by telling ChatGPT who to be, what to do, what to avoid, what to copy, and what to deliver.

If your prompts have been a little too loose, try POWER on something you already do: an email, a lesson plan, a social post, a weekly meal plan, or a tricky text message you would rather not overthink for 45 minutes. Start simple, test one prompt today, and watch how much better the answer gets when you stop being vague and start being specific.
