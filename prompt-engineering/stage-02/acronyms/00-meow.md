# MEOW

MEOW is a prompt-writing shortcut for getting better answers without turning your prompt into a legal contract. It stands for **Mission, Examples, Output, Warnings**. In plain English, it means: tell the AI what you want, show it what good looks like, specify the kind of answer you need, and point out what to avoid.

It is called **MEOW**, which is memorable, slightly ridiculous, and honestly useful. If your prompts keep giving you something close-ish but not quite right, MEOW helps fix that.

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **M, Mission** | Goal | States the main job you want the AI to do |
| **E, Examples** | Examples | Shows the style, pattern, or quality you want |
| **O, Output** | Result | Defines the format or type of answer you want back |
| **W, Warnings** | Guardrails | Tells the AI what to avoid, be careful about, or not assume |

## Why this template works

MEOW belongs to **Controlled Prompting**, which is the stage where you stop asking vague questions and start guiding the answer on purpose.

A lot of weak prompts fail for predictable reasons:

- The goal is fuzzy
- The answer format is not specified
- The AI has no example to imitate
- There are no guardrails, so it fills gaps with guesses

MEOW solves all four.

**Mission** gives direction.  
**Examples** reduce ambiguity.  
**Output** makes the response usable.  
**Warnings** prevent messy, off-target, or overconfident answers.

In other words, MEOW helps you stop getting “technically an answer” and start getting “actually helpful.”

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> **MISSION:** Create a professional email to a client explaining that their project deadline must move back by one week due to a supplier delay.  
> **EXAMPLES:** Use a tone similar to this: polite, direct, calm, and solution-focused. Example phrase: “We want to keep you informed and offer a clear next step.”  
> **OUTPUT:** Write 2 versions. Each version should be 120 to 150 words and include a subject line.  
> **WARNINGS:** Do not sound defensive. Do not blame the supplier too aggressively. Do not promise an exact delivery date unless stated. Avoid jargon.

**How the template shows up:**
- **M, Mission:** The task is clear, write a professional delay email to a client.
- **E, Examples:** The prompt includes a sample tone and a model phrase.
- **O, Output:** The response must be 2 versions, each 120 to 150 words, with subject lines.
- **W, Warnings:** The AI is told what tone problems and risky claims to avoid.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version is strong because it removes guessing. The AI knows the purpose, the tone, the format, and the danger zones. Without MEOW, you might get an email that is too casual, too vague, or weirdly dramatic, like a soap opera character announcing office stationery delays. With MEOW, the result is much more business-ready.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> Help me write a short LinkedIn post about finishing a team training program.  
>  
> - Goal: sound confident and appreciative, not braggy  
> - Example style: “Proud of what our team accomplished together, and excited to keep learning”  
> - Output: 3 post options, each under 80 words  
> - Avoid: clichés, hashtags overload, and cheesy motivational language

**How the template shows up:**
- **M, Mission:** The mission is to write a LinkedIn post about completing a team training program.
- **E, Examples:** The example sentence gives the AI a target style.
- **O, Output:** The answer should be 3 options, each under 80 words.
- **W, Warnings:** The AI is told to avoid clichés, too many hashtags, and cheesy language.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt is less formal, but still controlled. The example gives the AI a tone anchor, which is often the difference between polished and painfully corporate. The output limit keeps the response practical for LinkedIn, and the warnings block the usual fluff. This is the kind of prompt that saves you from posts that sound like they were written by a management poster in a break room.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> Write me a simple recipe for a quick vegetarian dinner using pasta, spinach, and canned tomatoes. Make it beginner-friendly and keep the instructions short. Something like a basic weeknight meal, not a fancy restaurant dish. Format it as ingredients plus steps, and do not include anything hard to find or a long explanation.

**How the template shows up:**
- **M, Mission:** The mission is to create a simple vegetarian dinner recipe with specific ingredients.
- **E, Examples:** “Something like a basic weeknight meal” acts as a style example and sets expectations.
- **O, Output:** The user asks for ingredients plus steps, with short instructions.
- **W, Warnings:** The AI is told not to make it fancy, not to use hard-to-find ingredients, and not to add a long explanation.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
Even though this sounds casual, it still uses MEOW. That is the beauty of it. You do not have to label every part for the method to work. The prompt gives enough direction to produce something realistic and useful. Without these cues, the AI might hand you a recipe involving saffron, three pans, and emotional resilience. With MEOW, dinner stays doable.

## Warnings and extra tips

MEOW is helpful, but it is not magic. A few things to watch for:

### 1. Do not make the mission vague
If your mission is “write something good,” you are basically asking the AI to read your mind. It cannot. At least not yet, and frankly that is for the best.

### 2. Examples should guide, not trap
Examples are powerful, but if you give one very specific example, the AI may copy its structure too closely. If you want variety, say so. For example: “Use this as a tone reference, not as a template.”

### 3. Output needs enough detail
If you want bullets, a table, a script, an email, or a checklist, say it. “Tell me about this” often leads to a blob of text. Blobs are rarely anyone’s dream format.

### 4. Warnings are essential when accuracy matters
Warnings are especially useful when you want the AI to avoid:
- making up facts
- sounding too confident
- giving risky advice
- using the wrong tone
- adding details you did not ask for

For example, you can say:
- “If uncertain, say what is missing.”
- “Do not invent statistics.”
- “Avoid legal or medical advice.”
- “Do not assume my audience already knows the topic.”

### 5. MEOW is not always enough by itself
Sometimes you also need:
- a specific audience
- a role or persona
- limits like word count
- background context

MEOW is a great control tool, but for more advanced prompts, you may combine it with other ingredients like role, audience, or tone.

### 6. Keep it practical
The best prompts are not the longest. They are the clearest. Use enough detail to guide the answer, not so much that your prompt starts looking like tax paperwork.

## Quick summary

**MEOW = Mission, Examples, Output, Warnings.**  
It helps you tell the AI:
- what to do,
- what good looks like,
- what kind of answer to give,
- and what to avoid.

That combination makes prompts clearer, more reliable, and much easier to reuse.

A good next step: try MEOW on something you already do, like writing an email, planning a meal, creating a social post, or summarizing a meeting. Start simple. Write one prompt with all four parts, then compare the result to your usual prompt. Odds are, the difference will be loud enough to hear over your inner skeptic.
