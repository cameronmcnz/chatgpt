# Hook

A **hook** is the part of a prompt that gets attention fast. It is the opening line that makes the AI immediately understand the tone, urgency, or angle of what you want. Think of it like the first line of an email subject, a headline, or the opening scene of a movie. If the start is flat, the rest can feel flat too. Prompting is no different.

## What this term means

In prompt engineering, a **hook** is the opening that grabs attention. It sets the stage for what comes next and gives the model an immediate signal about the direction of the response.

A hook does not have to be flashy or dramatic. This is not late-night infomercial territory. It just needs to be clear and purposeful. A good hook tells the AI, right away, what matters most.

For example, compare these two openings:

- "Write something about productivity."
- "Write a punchy intro for a productivity guide aimed at burnt-out managers."

The second one has a stronger hook. It points the AI toward a sharper, more focused response.

In practice, a hook often appears at the beginning of a prompt and frames the rest of the request. It can create urgency, spark curiosity, define the audience, or establish tone.

## Why it improves a prompt

A hook improves a prompt because it helps the model lock onto your intent earlier. Instead of wandering into a vague answer, the AI gets a stronger opening signal.

Here is what a hook helps with:

- **Focus**: It tells the AI what the central angle is.
- **Tone**: It can signal serious, playful, urgent, persuasive, or informative.
- **Relevance**: It helps the response start in the right place instead of warming up for three paragraphs first.
- **Engagement**: It makes the output sound more compelling, especially for marketing, writing, teaching, and presentations.

A prompt without a hook can still work, but it may feel generic. A prompt with a good hook often produces output that feels more intentional and better aimed. Basically, it gives the AI fewer chances to wander off like a meeting that should have been an email.

## Similar words you might see

You may also see people use similar terms such as:

- **Opening**
- **Headline**
- **Attention grabber**

These are close cousins of **hook**. The exact wording changes, but the idea stays the same: start strong so the rest of the prompt has direction.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1

> Write a blog post opening with a strong hook for an article about why small businesses waste money on software they never use. Make it sound direct, modern, and slightly witty.

This prompt uses the term by explicitly asking for a **strong hook** at the start of the blog post. It tells the AI that the opening should grab attention before moving into the topic.

Using a hook improves the prompt because it pushes the response toward a sharper introduction instead of a bland one. Rather than beginning with something forgettable like "Software is important for businesses," the AI is more likely to create an opening with energy and purpose.

### Sample prompt 2

> Create three headline-style hooks for a LinkedIn post about employees feeling overwhelmed by too many meetings. Keep them professional, concise, and relatable.

This prompt uses the idea of a hook through the phrase **headline-style hooks**. It asks for short openings designed to catch interest quickly.

Using the hook here improves the prompt because it narrows the task to attention-grabbing openings, not a full post. That makes the output more usable if you want to test different ways to begin. It is especially helpful for social media, where people scroll like they are being chased.

### Sample prompt 3

> Start this training email with an attention-grabbing opening that makes employees want to keep reading. The topic is password security, and the tone should be clear, practical, and not preachy.

This prompt uses a synonym of hook, **attention-grabbing opening**, to define what the first lines should do. It also gives topic and tone, which helps the AI shape the hook appropriately.

Using the term improves the prompt because it tells the AI not to bury the point under a dull introduction. For a topic like password security, that matters. Nobody wakes up hoping to read another compliance email, so the opening has to do some actual work.

## Quick summary

A **hook** is the opening that grabs attention and points the response in the right direction. Start strong, and the rest of the prompt usually behaves better, kind of like a meeting with an agenda and coffee.
