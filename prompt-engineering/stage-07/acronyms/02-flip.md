# FLIP

FLIP is what you use when a prompt feels a little too agreeable.

It helps you challenge an idea instead of just polishing it. If a normal prompt asks, "What sounds good?", FLIP asks, "What could go wrong, what are we missing, and what else should we consider?" Think of it as turning the proposal over before you buy it, like checking the bottom of a used office chair before trusting your spine to it.

FLIP belongs to the research, verification, and critical thinking stage. In plain English, this is the quality-control layer. AI can sound smooth, confident, and completely incorrect. FLIP helps you pressure-test the answer.

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| F, Find the opposite | Critical Thinking | Forces the model to consider the reverse position or competing viewpoint. |
| L, List objections | Trade-offs | Surfaces concerns, risks, and reasons someone might disagree. |
| I, Identify weaknesses | Critical Thinking | Looks for flaws, blind spots, unsupported assumptions, or weak logic. |
| P, Propose alternatives | Decision | Moves beyond criticism and suggests better options or fallback plans. |

## Why this template works

A lot of weak prompts ask AI to help with an idea, then accidentally encourage it to cheerlead. That is great if you want encouragement. It is less great if you are making a real decision.

FLIP works because it deliberately interrupts agreement.

Instead of asking for one polished answer, you ask for tension:
- the opposite view,
- the objections,
- the weak spots,
- and alternatives.

That makes the result more balanced and more useful. You are not just getting a shiny answer. You are getting a stress test.

This is especially helpful when you are:
- evaluating a business idea,
- comparing tools,
- making a policy decision,
- reviewing a plan,
- checking a recommendation before acting on it.

In short, FLIP helps you think like a skeptical editor instead of an easily impressed audience member.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> FIND THE OPPOSITE: Argue against the idea that our small business should switch to a four-day workweek next quarter.  
> LIST OBJECTIONS: Provide the strongest objections related to staffing, customer service, scheduling, and profitability.  
> IDENTIFY WEAKNESSES: Point out assumptions, missing data, and practical weaknesses in the proposal.  
> PROPOSE ALTERNATIVES: Suggest three lower-risk alternatives we could test before making a full switch.  
> FORMAT: Use clear headings and a short final recommendation.

**How the template shows up:**
- **F, Find the opposite:** The prompt explicitly asks the model to argue against the four-day workweek idea.
- **L, List objections:** It requests specific objections in staffing, customer service, scheduling, and profitability.
- **I, Identify weaknesses:** It asks for assumptions, missing data, and practical weaknesses.
- **P, Propose alternatives:** It asks for three lower-risk alternatives before a full switch.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version is strong because it removes ambiguity. The model is not left guessing whether you want support or critique. It is told to challenge the proposal from multiple angles, then offer practical next steps. That usually produces a much better decision-making response than, "What do you think about a four-day workweek?" which often leads to a pleasant but shallow answer.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> I am considering using AI chatbots for customer support in an online store. Help me pressure-test the idea.  
> - Show me the opposite case, why this might be a bad move.  
> - List the biggest objections customers, staff, or management might raise.  
> - Identify weak points in the plan, especially around cost, quality, and trust.  
> - Propose two or three alternatives, including one option that uses less automation.  
> Keep the response concise and practical.

**How the template shows up:**
- **F, Find the opposite:** "Show me the opposite case" asks the model to argue against adopting AI chatbots.
- **L, List objections:** It asks for objections from customers, staff, and management.
- **I, Identify weaknesses:** It asks for weak points involving cost, quality, and trust.
- **P, Propose alternatives:** It asks for several alternatives, including one with less automation.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version feels more natural, but it still keeps the FLIP structure. That balance is useful in real life. You get a critical review without having to write a stiff, corporate prompt that sounds like it was approved by three committees and one passive-aggressive email thread. The structure also makes the response more actionable because it covers both risks and options.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> I am thinking about replacing our weekly team meeting with a shared written update. Before you tell me why that sounds efficient, tell me why it might fail, what people will complain about, where the weak spots are, and what other options we should try first.

**How the template shows up:**
- **F, Find the opposite:** "Tell me why it might fail" asks for the opposing case.
- **L, List objections:** "What people will complain about" pulls out objections from stakeholders.
- **I, Identify weaknesses:** "Where the weak spots are" directly asks for weaknesses.
- **P, Propose alternatives:** "What other options we should try first" asks for alternatives before committing.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This is casual, but still smart. It nudges the model away from instant agreement and toward critical analysis. That is the magic of FLIP. You do not always need labels. You just need to ask for the right kinds of pressure. This style works well when you want a fast answer that still has some backbone.

## Warnings and extra tips

FLIP is powerful, but it can be overused.

If you use it on every tiny task, you can turn a simple prompt into a courtroom drama. Not everything needs a cross-examination. Sometimes you just need a clean summary, a draft email, or dinner ideas that do not involve a committee review.

A few things to watch for:

- **Do not ask for criticism without context.** If the model does not know your goals, budget, audience, or constraints, its objections may be generic.
- **Do not confuse skepticism with accuracy.** A critical answer can still be wrong. FLIP improves reasoning, but it does not guarantee facts.
- **Ask for evidence when stakes are high.** If the decision matters, pair FLIP with requests for sources, confidence levels, or assumptions.
- **Watch for fake balance.** Sometimes the opposite case is weak for a reason. FLIP is for testing ideas, not pretending every side is equally strong.
- **Make alternatives realistic.** If you ask for alternatives, give limits like budget, timeframe, or team size. Otherwise you may get dreamy suggestions that belong in a keynote slide, not real life.

A good upgrade is to combine FLIP with follow-up questions like:
- Which objection is most serious?
- What evidence would help confirm this risk?
- Which alternative is lowest cost?
- What assumptions should we test first?

That is where FLIP becomes genuinely useful, not just intellectually dramatic.

## Quick summary

FLIP stands for **Find the opposite, List objections, Identify weaknesses, Propose alternatives**.

It helps you challenge ideas instead of automatically accepting them. That means better prompts, better analysis, and fewer situations where the AI sounds confident and you later discover it was confidently wandering.

Try FLIP on something you are already deciding right now, a tool, a habit, a work process, or a purchase. Ask the model to argue against it, surface objections, expose weak spots, and suggest better options. Sometimes the best prompt is not the one that gets praise. It is the one that saves you from a bad idea.
