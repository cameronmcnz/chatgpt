# Practice

Practice is the part of a prompt where the user stops just reading and actually does something. Instead of asking the AI to explain a topic forever like a coworker trapped in a meeting with no agenda, you ask it to test, quiz, or rehearse with you. That turns passive learning into active learning, which is usually where the real progress shows up.

## What this term means

In prompt engineering, **Practice** means telling the AI to **test or rehearse with the user**.

That can look like:

- asking quiz questions,
- running flashcard-style recall,
- giving the user a scenario to respond to,
- asking the user to explain something back,
- checking answers before moving on.

The key idea is simple: the AI is not only delivering information, it is also helping the user **use** that information.

If you only ask for explanations, you may feel like you understand the topic. Practice is the part that reveals whether you actually do. Slightly humbling sometimes, but useful.

In lesson-style prompting, Practice often appears after teaching and examples. First the AI explains. Then it demonstrates. Then it asks the user to try.

## Why it improves a prompt

Practice improves a prompt because it changes the interaction from one-way output into a two-way learning process.

Here is what that adds:

- **Checks understanding**: A user may think they get it until they have to answer a question without looking.
- **Improves memory**: Active recall sticks better than rereading. Your brain likes to be mildly inconvenienced.
- **Reveals gaps**: Practice shows exactly where the confusion is.
- **Makes the prompt more interactive**: Instead of getting one giant answer, the AI can guide the user step by step.
- **Creates better feedback loops**: Once the user answers, the AI can correct mistakes and adjust difficulty.

In prompt engineering, this is valuable because it gives the model a clear job. Rather than "teach me everything about budgeting," you can say, "teach me the basics, then quiz me with five short questions, one at a time, and explain any answers I miss." That is much easier for the AI to do well, and much easier for the user to learn from.

Practice also works especially well when paired with frameworks like:

- **TUTOR**, where Practice appears in the **Test** step
- **QUIZ**, where Practice appears in the **Question** step
- **ACT**, where Practice appears in the **Test the user** step

So if you want the AI to act more like a coach and less like a talking encyclopedia, Practice is the lever to pull.

## Similar words you might see

Some terms mean nearly the same thing in prompts:

- **Quiz**
- **Test the user**
- **Question**
- **Active recall**

These all point toward the same basic move: ask the learner to respond, remember, solve, or demonstrate understanding.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1

> Teach me the basics of phishing emails in simple language. Then quiz me with 5 short questions, one at a time. After each answer, tell me if I am correct and briefly explain why.

This prompt uses **Practice** by explicitly asking the AI to **quiz** the user after teaching the topic. The user is not just reading about phishing emails, they are being asked to recall and apply what they learned.

Using Practice improves the prompt because it adds a built-in check for understanding. Instead of ending with a neat explanation and a false sense of confidence, the prompt creates a simple learning loop: teach, answer, correct, continue.

### Sample prompt 2

> Act as a customer service trainer. Create a realistic scenario where I need to respond to an upset customer. Let me write my reply first, then evaluate my response and give me a better version if needed.

This prompt uses Practice through **rehearsal**. The AI creates a scenario, then waits for the user to respond before giving feedback. That is not just information delivery, it is hands-on training.

Using Practice improves the prompt because it makes the interaction more realistic and useful. If the user only asked for customer service tips, they would get advice. By rehearsing, they get to try the skill in context, which is much closer to what they will need in real life.

### Sample prompt 3

> Help me study basic project management terms. Give me 6 flashcard-style active recall questions. Do not show the answers until I respond. If I miss one, explain it simply and ask a follow-up question on the same term.

This prompt uses Practice by asking for **active recall**. The AI is told to hold back the answers, wait for the user to respond, and reinforce weak areas with follow-up questions.

Using Practice improves the prompt because it creates structure and discipline. The model is not allowed to dump all the information at once. Instead, it helps the user retrieve knowledge from memory, which usually leads to better retention and clearer awareness of what still needs work.

## Quick summary

Practice turns a prompt from "tell me stuff" into "let's see if I can actually do this," which is where learning stops being decorative and starts being useful.
