# Scenario

A **scenario** in prompt engineering means you give the AI a simulated situation to work inside. Instead of asking for a generic answer, you set the scene. You create context, constraints, stakes, or a specific moment, and that usually gets you a much more useful response. Basically, you stop asking the AI to float in empty space and give it an actual room to stand in.

## What this term means

In prompt engineering, a **scenario** is a made-up but realistic situation that helps guide the AI's response.

You are telling the model something like:

- what is happening
- who is involved
- what the goal is
- what kind of environment or pressure exists

For example, asking, "How do I handle a customer complaint?" is broad. Asking, "Create a scenario where I am a store manager dealing with an angry customer whose online order arrived late, and help me respond professionally," gives the AI a setting to work with.

That extra setup matters. A scenario makes the prompt feel less like a trivia question and more like a scene in a movie, except with fewer car chases and more email drafting.

In prompt frameworks, this idea shows up directly in **SIM** and **ACT**:

- **SIM** = Scenario, Identity, Mission
- **ACT** = Assume role, Create scenario, Test the user

In both cases, the scenario gives structure to the interaction. It tells the AI what kind of world it is operating in.

## Why it improves a prompt

Using a scenario improves a prompt because it adds **context**.

Context helps the AI make better choices about:

- tone
- detail
- priorities
- realism
- relevance

Without a scenario, the AI may give a broad, safe, generic answer. That is not always wrong, but it can be about as exciting as office training from 1994.

With a scenario, you make the request more specific. You can simulate:

- a workplace conversation
- a customer service issue
- a coaching session
- a negotiation
- a learning exercise
- an emergency or high-pressure decision

This is especially helpful when you want the AI to:

- practice with you
- respond as if a situation is real
- adapt to constraints
- help you rehearse before a real interaction

A scenario also helps if you are learning. Instead of just reading advice, you can test ideas in a safe simulation. That makes the AI more like a practice partner and less like a very enthusiastic encyclopedia.

## Similar words you might see

These terms are often used in a similar way:

- **Simulation**
- **Roleplay**
- **Create scenario**

They are not always identical, but they are close cousins.

- **Simulation** often emphasizes realism and practice.
- **Roleplay** usually includes specific people or roles acting in the situation.
- **Create scenario** is the plain-English version, very direct and very usable.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1

> Create a scenario where I am a team lead giving constructive feedback to an employee who keeps missing deadlines. Roleplay the employee's likely responses, then help me practice a calm and professional reply.

This prompt uses **scenario** by setting up a specific workplace situation with a clear interpersonal challenge. It does not just ask for "feedback tips." It creates a realistic moment with roles, tension, and a goal.

Using the scenario improves the prompt because the AI can tailor the conversation to a believable setting. That makes the advice more practical, and it lets you rehearse language you might actually use in real life.

### Sample prompt 2

> Simulate a scenario in which I am preparing for a job interview for a project coordinator role. The interviewer is concerned that I have experience gaps. Ask me five interview questions based on that situation, one at a time, and wait for my answers.

This prompt uses **simulation** as a form of scenario. It creates a hiring situation, defines the concern, and tells the AI how to interact step by step.

Using the scenario improves the prompt because the AI knows what kind of questions to ask and what pressure point to focus on. Instead of random interview practice, you get targeted practice based on a realistic challenge.

### Sample prompt 3

> Create a customer service roleplay scenario where I am responding to a client whose subscription was canceled by mistake. The client is frustrated but not abusive. Write the client's opening message, then ask me to draft a reply and give feedback on my response.

This prompt uses **roleplay** and **create scenario** together. It defines the problem, the emotional tone, and the training task. That gives the AI a clear setup for an interactive exercise.

Using the scenario improves the prompt because it controls the situation more precisely. The AI can keep the conversation realistic, avoid making the customer unrealistically hostile, and give feedback that matches the actual challenge you are practicing.

## Quick summary

A **scenario** gives your prompt a setting, a purpose, and a bit of reality, because "help me with this" is fine, but "here is the situation" is where the useful stuff starts.
