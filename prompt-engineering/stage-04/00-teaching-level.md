# Teaching Level

Teaching Level is the part of a prompt that tells the AI how deep, simple, or advanced the explanation should be. It is less about *what* you want, and more about *how taught* you want it. Think of it as choosing between "give me the quick office version" and "walk me through this like I missed the meeting and the follow-up email."

## What this term means

In prompt engineering, **Teaching Level** means **adjusting explanation depth**.

That means you tell the AI how detailed the response should be, how much background to include, and how complex the language can get. If you do not specify this, the AI has to guess. Sometimes it guesses well. Sometimes it gives you a graduate seminar when you wanted a sticky note.

Teaching Level helps shape things like:

- how simple or technical the wording is
- how much context is included
- how many steps are explained
- whether examples are basic, layered, or expert-level
- how much prior knowledge the response assumes

This is especially useful when you are learning something new, teaching someone else, or switching between audiences. A manager, a new employee, and a specialist probably do not need the same version of the same answer.

In the **TUTOR** framework, the **T** stands for **Teach**, which maps directly to Teaching Level. It reminds you to decide how the material should be taught before worrying about examples, practice, or feedback.

## Why it improves a prompt

Teaching Level improves a prompt because it reduces ambiguity.

If you ask, "Explain machine learning," you might get something broad, technical, or oddly cheerful. If you ask, "Explain machine learning at a simple level using plain English and one example," you are much more likely to get a useful answer.

Using Teaching Level helps you:

- match the explanation to your current knowledge
- avoid answers that are too vague or too technical
- control how much detail you get
- make the output more usable for study, training, or decision-making
- get layered explanations that build understanding step by step

It also saves time. Instead of asking for a rewrite after the AI overshoots or undershoots, you set the expectation upfront. Fewer follow-up prompts, less digital herding of cats.

A good prompt can use Teaching Level in several ways:

- directly: "Explain this simply"
- by audience: "Explain this for a new employee"
- by depth: "Give a Level 1 overview, then an advanced version"
- by structure: "Start simple, then add technical detail"

That last one is especially helpful when you want a **layered** explanation. You get the easy version first, then the more advanced material after your brain has had a chance to put on its shoes.

## Similar words you might see

These terms often signal Teaching Level:

- **Simple**
- **Clear**
- **Advanced**
- **Layered**
- **Expert**
- **Level 1**

They all point to the same idea: controlling explanation depth so the AI teaches at the right level.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1

> Explain cloud computing at a Level 1 teaching level. Use plain language, avoid jargon, and include one everyday example.

This prompt uses Teaching Level explicitly by saying **Level 1** and reinforcing it with "plain language" and "avoid jargon." That gives the AI a clear target for explanation depth.

Why this improves the prompt: it prevents the response from getting too technical too fast. Instead of a wall of terms like infrastructure, virtualization, and distributed architecture, you are more likely to get a clean, beginner-friendly explanation that actually helps.

### Sample prompt 2

> Teach me the difference between revenue and profit using a layered explanation. Start with a simple two-sentence definition, then give a clearer business example, then finish with an advanced explanation for someone running a small company.

This prompt uses Teaching Level through the word **layered** and by breaking the explanation into increasing levels of depth. It does not force the AI to pick one audience only. It asks for a progression.

Why this improves the prompt: it makes the answer more flexible and easier to learn from. You get the basics first, then context, then the more advanced version. That is far more useful than getting dropped into accounting language like it is episode 47 of a series you never started.

### Sample prompt 3

> I am preparing training for new team members. Explain phishing in a clear teaching style first, then provide an expert version I can use in manager notes. Include the same core idea in both versions.

This prompt uses Teaching Level by requesting two versions: one **clear** and one **expert**. It also keeps them aligned by asking for the same core idea in both versions.

Why this improves the prompt: it makes the output practical for real work. You can use one explanation for learners and another for leadership, without getting inconsistent definitions. That saves editing time and makes the training more coherent.

## Quick summary

Teaching Level tells the AI how deeply to explain something, from simple and clear to layered and expert. Use it when you want answers that match your knowledge, your audience, and your patience level. Because "explain it better" is a strategy, but "explain it at Level 1, then build up" is a plan.
