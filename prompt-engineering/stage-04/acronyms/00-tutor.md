# TUTOR

TUTOR is a prompt pattern for turning AI into a decent coach instead of a weirdly confident encyclopedia. It helps you ask for teaching, examples, practice, mistake-checking, and correction, all in one prompt.

If you want ChatGPT to actually help you learn something, not just dump information on your screen like a fax machine from 1994, TUTOR is a solid structure.

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **T, Teach** | **Teaching Level** | Tells the AI how deeply or simply to explain the topic. |
| **U, Use examples** | **Examples** | Adds concrete illustrations so the lesson makes sense in real life. |
| **T, Test** | **Practice** | Makes the AI check your understanding with questions or exercises. |
| **O, Observe mistakes** | **Evaluation** | Tells the AI to spot errors, misunderstandings, or weak areas. |
| **R, Remediate** | **Feedback** | Instructs the AI to correct mistakes and explain how to improve. |

## Why this template works

A lot of beginners write prompts like, “Explain budgeting,” or “Teach me Excel.” That can work, sort of. But it often produces a wall of information with no interaction, no check for understanding, and no correction.

TUTOR fixes that by making the learning process active.

Instead of only asking for information, you ask for a mini lesson flow:

1. Teach the concept at the right level.
2. Show examples.
3. Test your understanding.
4. Observe what you got wrong.
5. Remediate with feedback.

That sequence matters. It mirrors how a good human tutor works. First they explain, then they demonstrate, then they check, then they correct. Revolutionary, I know.

TUTOR is especially useful when you want ChatGPT to help with:
- learning a new skill
- studying for a test
- practicing job interview answers
- improving writing
- learning software tools
- reviewing mistakes after a quiz or exercise

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> TEACH: Explain the basics of creating a household budget at a beginner level using simple language.  
> USE EXAMPLES: Include 3 realistic monthly budgeting examples for a single adult, a family of four, and a college student.  
> TEST: After the lesson, give me 5 practice questions based on the examples.  
> OBSERVE MISTAKES: After I answer, identify any errors, misunderstandings, or weak areas in my thinking.  
> REMEDIATE: Correct my mistakes and give a short improvement plan with tips for avoiding the same errors next time.

**How the template shows up:**
- **Teach:** “Explain the basics of creating a household budget at a beginner level using simple language.”
- **Use examples:** “Include 3 realistic monthly budgeting examples...”
- **Test:** “Give me 5 practice questions...”
- **Observe mistakes:** “Identify any errors, misunderstandings, or weak areas...”
- **Remediate:** “Correct my mistakes and give a short improvement plan...”

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt does more than ask for an explanation. It creates a full learning loop. The teaching level keeps the lesson beginner-friendly. The examples make budgeting concrete instead of abstract. The test turns passive reading into active practice. The mistake observation helps the AI act like a coach, not just a lecturer. The remediation step ensures you do not just hear “wrong,” but also learn what to do next.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> Help me learn how to write a professional email to a customer.  
> - Teach it at an intermediate level, clear but not overly technical.  
> - Show 2 good examples and 1 bad example with a quick explanation of why it fails.  
> - Then give me a short email scenario and ask me to write the reply.  
> - Review my response for tone, clarity, and professionalism.  
> - Then rewrite my email and explain the fixes.

**How the template shows up:**
- **Teach:** “Teach it at an intermediate level, clear but not overly technical.”
- **Use examples:** “Show 2 good examples and 1 bad example...”
- **Test:** “Give me a short email scenario and ask me to write the reply.”
- **Observe mistakes:** “Review my response for tone, clarity, and professionalism.”
- **Remediate:** “Rewrite my email and explain the fixes.”

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version feels more natural, but it still has all five TUTOR parts. That makes the result far more useful than simply saying, “Teach me business email.” The examples create contrast, especially the bad example, which often teaches faster than a polished one. The scenario adds practice. The review step catches issues you might not notice. The rewrite gives you a better model to copy next time. It is basically getting a writing coach without scheduling a meeting.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> Teach me the difference between “your” and “you’re” like I keep mixing them up in emails. Start simple, show me a few examples, then quiz me with short sentences. After I answer, point out what I got wrong and help me remember the rule with an easy trick.

**How the template shows up:**
- **Teach:** “Teach me the difference... Start simple...”
- **Use examples:** “Show me a few examples...”
- **Test:** “Quiz me with short sentences.”
- **Observe mistakes:** “Point out what I got wrong...”
- **Remediate:** “Help me remember the rule with an easy trick.”

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This is casual, short, and still very effective. It does not use rigid labels, but the teaching flow is still there. The simple explanation prevents overload. The examples clarify the rule. The quiz checks whether you actually understand it. The mistake review catches confusion in context. The memory trick adds remediation, which is often the difference between “I saw it once” and “I can use it tomorrow.”

## Warnings and extra tips

TUTOR is strong, but it is not magic. A few things to watch:

- **Do not skip the teaching level.** If you do, the AI may explain at the wrong depth. Too simple is annoying. Too advanced is also annoying, just with bigger words.
- **Ask for realistic examples.** Vague examples can make the lesson feel fake or too generic.
- **Make the test appropriate.** If you ask for 50 quiz questions on a basic topic, congratulations, you invented homework nobody wanted.
- **Be specific about what should be evaluated.** “Observe mistakes” works better when you name what matters, such as accuracy, tone, logic, grammar, or confidence.
- **Ask for corrective feedback, not just criticism.** Good remediation should explain the problem and show how to fix it.
- **Use follow-up rounds.** TUTOR works best interactively. After the AI teaches and tests you, answer the questions and continue the loop.
- **Know when TUTOR is not enough.** If you need a roleplay, interview simulation, or a realistic scenario, you may need to combine TUTOR with scenario-based prompting.
- **Check important facts.** For high-stakes learning, especially legal, medical, financial, or technical topics, verify the content from reliable sources.

A final practical tip: if the AI gives too much at once, tell it to go one step at a time. For example, ask it to teach first, then wait for you before moving to the test. That usually makes the learning experience much better.

## Quick summary

TUTOR helps you build prompts that actually teach:

- **Teach** at the right level
- **Use examples** to make it clear
- **Test** understanding
- **Observe mistakes** to find gaps
- **Remediate** with feedback and correction

If you want AI to act more like a tutor and less like a search result with confidence issues, TUTOR is a great structure to practice.

Try it next with something small and useful, like learning a spreadsheet formula, improving a work email, or fixing a grammar habit you keep repeating. Pick one topic, write a TUTOR prompt, and let the AI teach you step by step.
