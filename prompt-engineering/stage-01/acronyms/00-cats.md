# CATS

CATS is a simple prompt recipe for getting clearer answers without writing a novel first. It stands for **Context, Audience, Tone, Structure**. In other words: tell ChatGPT what is going on, who the answer is for, how it should sound, and what shape it should take.

If your prompts sometimes feel like tossing a sticky note into the void and hoping for magic, CATS gives that note a lot more direction.

## What the template means

| Letter | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| C | Context | Gives the background, situation, or facts ChatGPT needs before answering. |
| A | Audience | Defines who the response is for, so the explanation fits the right people. |
| T | Tone | Controls how the response sounds, such as friendly, formal, calm, persuasive, or direct. |
| S | Structure | Tells ChatGPT how to organize the answer, such as bullets, steps, table, email, or short summary. |

## Why this template works

CATS works because most weak prompts are missing one or more of these pieces.

People often ask for a result but forget the setup. They say things like, “Write an email,” or “Explain this topic,” and then wonder why the answer feels generic. That is not ChatGPT being dramatic. That is just a prompt with missing instructions.

Here is what CATS fixes:

- **Context** reduces guessing.
- **Audience** prevents answers that are too technical, too basic, or just weirdly mismatched.
- **Tone** keeps the style appropriate for the situation.
- **Structure** makes the output easier to use right away.

Together, these four pieces help you ask more clearly, which usually leads to more useful first drafts and fewer frustrating follow-up corrections.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> CONTEXT: I manage a small office, and our team is switching from paper timesheets to a digital time-tracking app next month. Some employees are nervous about the change.  
> AUDIENCE: Employees with mixed levels of technical comfort.  
> TONE: Clear, professional, reassuring, and respectful.  
> STRUCTURE: Write a short workplace announcement email with a subject line, opening paragraph, 3 bullet points explaining the change, and a polite closing.

**How the template shows up:**
- **C, Context:** The prompt explains the workplace situation, the switch to digital time tracking, and the concern employees have.
- **A, Audience:** It identifies the readers as employees with mixed comfort levels around technology.
- **T, Tone:** It asks for a clear, professional, reassuring, and respectful voice.
- **S, Structure:** It specifies the exact output format: subject line, opening paragraph, 3 bullet points, and a closing.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version gives ChatGPT enough information to write something usable on the first try. Without context, the email might feel vague. Without audience, it might sound too technical or too simplistic. Without tone, it could come off cold or overly cheerful, which is a special kind of corporate chaos. Without structure, you would probably get a wall of text and then have to reorganize it yourself.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> I need a simple explanation of phishing scams for a community workshop.  
> - Background: The attendees are adults who use email and text regularly but are not cybersecurity experts.  
> - Audience: Beginners who want practical advice.  
> - Tone: Friendly, calm, and non-alarmist.  
> - Structure: Give me a 5-part outline with a short example for each part, plus a final checklist of warning signs.

**How the template shows up:**
- **C, Context:** The prompt states this is for a community workshop about phishing scams.
- **A, Audience:** It says the attendees are beginners and not cybersecurity experts.
- **T, Tone:** It asks for a friendly, calm, non-alarmist style.
- **S, Structure:** It requests a 5-part outline, short examples, and a final checklist.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt is less formal, but it still uses the full CATS framework. That means the answer is more likely to match the real teaching setting. The context keeps the content focused on phishing instead of wandering into every online scam known to humanity. The audience instruction keeps jargon under control. The tone prevents fear-based wording. The structure creates something that can be turned into a workshop handout fast.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> I am helping my dad learn how to use online grocery ordering for the first time. Please explain how it works in a warm, patient way, and lay it out as a short step-by-step guide with a few tips for avoiding mistakes.

**How the template shows up:**
- **C, Context:** The situation is helping someone use online grocery ordering for the first time.
- **A, Audience:** The implied audience is a beginner learner who may need a simple explanation.
- **T, Tone:** The prompt asks for a warm, patient explanation.
- **S, Structure:** It asks for a short step-by-step guide with a few tips.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This one sounds natural, like something a real person would actually type, which is great. You do not have to sound like you are filing a report with mission control. Even casually, CATS still gives enough direction to make the answer useful. The response will likely be simpler, kinder, and more organized than if you had just typed, “Explain online grocery ordering.”

## Warnings and extra tips

CATS is great for beginner prompts, but it is not magic glitter.

A few things to watch out for:

- **Do not make the context too thin.** If important facts are missing, ChatGPT will fill in the blanks, and sometimes it fills them in with confidence that should probably be studied in a lab.
- **Do not overload the prompt with unnecessary backstory.** Context should help, not become a memoir.
- **Audience matters more than many people realize.** “Explain taxes” for a small business owner is different from “explain taxes” for a teenager with a first job.
- **Tone can conflict with task.** If you ask for a serious safety warning in a goofy tone, expect mixed results.
- **Structure should match the goal.** A table is great for comparison. A numbered list is great for steps. An email format is great if you actually need an email.
- **CATS may not be enough for advanced tasks.** If you need strict limits, examples, role instructions, or multi-step reasoning, you may need to add more prompt ingredients beyond CATS.
- **You can ask for revision.** If the first answer is close but not quite right, keep the same CATS setup and adjust one part. For example, change the tone, simplify the audience level, or tighten the structure.

A useful habit is to quickly check your draft prompt and ask:
- Did I explain the situation?
- Did I say who this is for?
- Did I say how it should sound?
- Did I say what form I want back?

If yes, your prompt is already in better shape than most.

## Quick summary

CATS stands for **Context, Audience, Tone, Structure**. It helps you write clearer prompts by giving ChatGPT the background, target reader, voice, and format it needs to produce a better answer.

Short version: **set the scene, name the reader, choose the voice, shape the output.**

Try CATS next time you ask for an email, explanation, summary, or set of instructions. Pick one everyday task, rewrite your prompt using CATS, and compare the result. That tiny upgrade is often the difference between “meh” and “actually useful.”
