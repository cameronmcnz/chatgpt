# Prompt Engineering Terms and Templates

Prompt engineering sounds more intimidating than it really is.

At its core, prompt engineering is the skill of giving ChatGPT clear instructions so it can give you better answers. That is it. No lab coat required. No glowing server room. No need to whisper "neural network" while looking thoughtfully out a window.

But there is a catch.

If you do not know the basic ingredients of a good prompt, ChatGPT can still answer, but the answer may be vague, generic, too long, too short, too weird, or somehow all of those at the same time. It is like asking someone to "make it better" without telling them what "better" means. That is not communication. That is workplace improv.

This section is designed to fix that.

## What this section is about

This part of the course teaches the core vocabulary of prompt engineering.

You will learn the terms that show up again and again in good prompts, including:

- Task
- Goal
- Context
- Audience
- Tone
- Format
- Result
- Role
- Limits
- Guardrails
- Examples
- Input material
- Summarization
- Organization
- Practice
- Evaluation
- Feedback
- Refinement
- Evidence
- Confidence

These words are not just vocabulary words for a quiz you will forget by Thursday.

They are prompt ingredients.

Once you understand them, you can build stronger prompts on purpose instead of just typing a sentence and hoping ChatGPT reads your mind like a psychic with Wi-Fi.

## Why these terms matter

Every good prompt has structure, even when it looks casual.

When you ask ChatGPT to do something useful, you are usually giving it several pieces of information:

- What you want it to do
- Why you want it done
- Who the answer is for
- What style or tone you want
- What information it should use
- What limits it should follow
- What format the answer should come back in

Those pieces have names.

A **task** tells ChatGPT what to do.  
A **goal** tells ChatGPT what you are trying to accomplish.  
**Context** gives it the background.  
**Audience** tells it who the answer is for.  
**Tone** tells it how the answer should sound.  
**Format** tells it how to arrange the response.  
**Guardrails** tell it what to avoid.  
**Examples** show it what good looks like.

Once you know these terms, you can diagnose weak prompts much faster.

A bad prompt is often not bad because the idea is bad. It is bad because something is missing.

Maybe the task is unclear.  
Maybe the goal is vague.  
Maybe the audience is not defined.  
Maybe the tone is wrong.  
Maybe there are no limits.  
Maybe the format is missing.  
Maybe the prompt is basically just yelling "help" into the machine and hoping for Shakespeare.

The terms give you a way to fix that.

## What the prompt templates are for

The prompt templates are reusable prompt frameworks.

They help you remember what to include when you want a better answer from ChatGPT.

Instead of staring at a blank prompt box like it just insulted your family, you can use a framework.

For example:

| Template | What it helps with |
|---|---|
| CATS | Context, Audience, Tone, Structure |
| CRAP | Context, Restrictions, Aim, Persona |
| FAST | Format, Audience, Style, Task |
| MEOW | Mission, Examples, Output, Warnings |
| PAWS | Persona, Ask, Warnings, Structure |
| TREAT | Task, Role, Examples, Audience, Tone |
| POWER | Persona, Objective, Warnings, Examples, Result |
| SNAP | Summarize, Narrow, Apply, Polish |
| CHUNK | Breaking large content into useful pieces |
| TUTOR | Teaching, testing, observing mistakes, and remediation |
| LOOP | Reviewing, improving, and prompting again |
| VERIFY | Checking evidence, assumptions, red flags, and confidence |
| DEBATE | Testing ideas from multiple sides before reaching a conclusion |

Some prompt templates are simple and beginner-friendly. Others are better for analysis, critique, research, revision, or advanced workflows.

The point is not to memorize every template like you are cramming for a 1980s geography test with a No. 2 pencil and a questionable amount of coffee.

The point is to build a toolkit.

When you need a clear prompt, use one framework.  
When you need feedback, use another.  
When you need research verification, use another.  
When you need a creative scenario, use another.

Different prompting problems need different prompting tools.

## How this helps you become a better prompt engineer

Prompt engineering is not about writing long prompts.

It is about writing intentional prompts.

A long prompt can still be terrible. Anyone can write a confusing wall of text. Many meetings are built on this proud tradition.

A good prompt gives ChatGPT the right information at the right time.

This section helps you become more intentional by teaching you how to:

- Identify the missing ingredient in a weak prompt
- Add context without overloading the request
- Set useful limits without strangling creativity
- Choose the right tone and audience
- Ask for better formats
- Use examples to guide the answer
- Turn rough ideas into structured prompts
- Use feedback loops to improve responses
- Ask ChatGPT to verify, critique, compare, and refine

That is where the real skill develops.

You stop thinking, "ChatGPT gave me a bad answer."

You start thinking, "My prompt did not give ChatGPT the right task, role, audience, format, or constraints."

That shift is huge.

Because once you can diagnose the prompt, you can improve the result.

## The stages of learning

The terms and prompt templates are organized in stages so you can build skill gradually.

You start with beginner foundations, where the goal is simply to make your prompt clear. Then you move into controlled prompting, where you learn to shape the answer with roles, limits, guardrails, and examples.

After that, you work with real material, such as notes, documents, transcripts, and messy content. Then you learn how to create teaching prompts, practice prompts, simulations, and roleplays.

From there, you move into improvement loops, where ChatGPT becomes something you revise with, not just something you ask once and abandon dramatically.

Finally, you get into analysis, decision-making, research, verification, evidence, confidence, and critical thinking.

In other words, you move from:

"I asked ChatGPT a question."

to:

"I designed a prompt that gives ChatGPT the context, role, constraints, format, examples, and evaluation criteria needed to produce a useful result."

That is a very different level of control.

## Why this matters in real life

These skills are useful almost everywhere.

You can use them to:

- Write clearer emails
- Summarize documents
- Create study guides
- Generate lesson plans
- Build content outlines
- Compare options
- Improve resumes and cover letters
- Plan projects
- Analyze ideas
- Brainstorm creative concepts
- Review writing
- Build workflows
- Learn difficult topics
- Ask better follow-up questions

The better your prompt, the better your odds of getting something useful back.

That does not mean ChatGPT will always be right. It will not. Sometimes it sounds confident while being wrong, which, to be fair, is also a popular human skill.

That is why later stages include evidence, verification, assumptions, confidence, and critical thinking.

A good prompt engineer does not just ask for answers.

A good prompt engineer asks for better reasoning, clearer structure, useful limits, and appropriate verification.

## How to use this section

You can work through the terms and prompt templates in order, or you can treat this section like a reference library.

When a prompt feels weak, ask yourself:

- Did I define the task?
- Did I explain the goal?
- Did I give enough context?
- Did I identify the audience?
- Did I ask for the right tone?
- Did I specify the format?
- Did I include examples?
- Did I add useful guardrails?
- Did I ask ChatGPT to review or improve the answer?
- Did I ask for evidence when accuracy matters?

If the answer is no, congratulations. You found the problem.

That is not failure. That is editing.

And prompt engineering is mostly good editing with a slightly shinier name.
