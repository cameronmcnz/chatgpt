# Perspective Shift

Perspective Shift is exactly what it sounds like: asking the AI to look at the same topic from a different viewpoint. It is one of the easiest ways to get better answers, especially when your first prompt gives you something flat, obvious, or painfully generic. Sometimes the fix is not "ask harder." It is "ask from another angle."

## What this term means

In prompt engineering, **Perspective Shift** means telling the AI to examine an idea, problem, decision, or piece of writing from a different point of view.

That point of view might be:

- a different person
- a different role
- a different audience
- a different stakeholder
- a different bias
- a different goal

For example, instead of asking, "Is this a good policy?" you could ask:

- "Explain this policy from an employee's perspective."
- "Now explain it from a manager's perspective."
- "What would a skeptical customer think?"
- "What bias might be shaping this argument?"

That shift changes the output. Same topic, different lens.

In practice, Perspective Shift helps the AI move beyond a single default explanation. Without it, the model often gives the most common, middle-of-the-road answer. Which is fine sometimes. But if you want nuance, conflict, empathy, criticism, or stronger decision-making, you need to make the viewpoint explicit.

## Why it improves a prompt

Perspective Shift improves a prompt because it adds **context and direction**.

When you specify a viewpoint, you help the AI answer questions like:

- Who is speaking?
- Who is affected?
- What matters most here?
- What assumptions might be hiding in the background?
- What objections or blind spots should be considered?

This makes prompts better in a few important ways:

1. **It creates more relevant answers.**  
   A response aimed at a customer should not sound like it was written for legal counsel. Unless your customer is legal counsel, in which case, congratulations on your very specific audience.

2. **It reveals trade-offs.**  
   One group may see a change as efficient, while another sees it as disruptive. Perspective Shift helps surface both.

3. **It reduces one-sided thinking.**  
   If you only ask for one viewpoint, you often get one worldview baked into the answer. Asking for another angle can expose bias or missing context.

4. **It improves decision-making.**  
   Comparing perspectives helps you spot risks, resistance, and opportunities before you act.

5. **It makes writing and analysis more human.**  
   Real people do not all think the same way. A good prompt should not pretend they do.

A simple way to think about it: Perspective Shift tells the AI which pair of glasses to wear.

## Similar words you might see

You may see this idea described with terms like:

- **Lens**
- **Look through**
- **Explain perspective**
- **Angle**
- **Note bias**

These all point to the same core move: change the viewpoint, then examine the topic again.

You may also see it inside prompt frameworks such as:

- **TILT**: Take another angle, Inspect, List trade-offs, Test assumptions
- **LENS**: Look through, Explain perspective, Note bias, Suggest insight

Both are useful because they turn "consider another viewpoint" into a repeatable prompt habit instead of a lucky accident.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1

> Explain remote work policies from three perspectives: an employee who wants flexibility, a manager responsible for team performance, and a CFO focused on cost control. Then summarize where their priorities conflict.

This prompt uses Perspective Shift by explicitly asking for three different viewpoints on the same issue. It does not let the AI stay in one bland, general explanation.

Using the term improves the prompt because it produces a more realistic analysis. Instead of getting a generic pros-and-cons list, you get competing priorities, which is usually where the actual decision lives.

### Sample prompt 2

> Review this marketing email through the lens of a skeptical customer. Explain what might feel unclear, pushy, or untrustworthy, and note any bias in the message.

This prompt uses Perspective Shift through the word **lens** and by naming a specific audience with a specific attitude: a skeptical customer.

That improves the prompt because it forces the AI to evaluate the message from outside the writer's assumptions. A draft that looks brilliant from the inside can look like late-night infomercial nonsense from the outside. Perspective Shift helps catch that.

### Sample prompt 3

> I am considering switching project management software. Use the TILT approach: take another angle by comparing the decision from the perspectives of the project manager, IT administrator, and end user; inspect the likely benefits and risks; list trade-offs; test assumptions I may be making.

This prompt uses Perspective Shift directly through the **take another angle** step in the TILT framework. It asks the AI to compare several stakeholder viewpoints before moving into evaluation.

Using the term improves the prompt because it creates structure and depth. Instead of a shallow software recommendation, the answer is more likely to reveal friction points, hidden costs, and false assumptions. In other words, fewer surprises after rollout, and fewer meetings that could have been emails.

## Quick summary

Perspective Shift helps you ask the AI, "What does this look like from somewhere else?" It is one of the fastest ways to get smarter, sharper, less one-note answers, like changing camera angles instead of staring at the same scene and hoping it gets interesting.
