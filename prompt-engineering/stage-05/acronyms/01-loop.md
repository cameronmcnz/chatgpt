# LOOP

LOOP is the prompt engineering reminder that your first AI answer is a draft, not a sacred artifact carved into stone tablets. You do not just ask once and hope for magic. You review, notice what is off, improve it, and ask again. That is how better prompting usually works.

In short: **LOOP helps you turn one prompt into a better conversation.**

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **L, Look** | Evaluation | Review the output and judge overall quality. Is it useful, accurate, clear, or on target? |
| **O, Observe** | Evaluation | Notice specific strengths and problems. What is missing, vague, too long, too formal, or just a little weird? |
| **O, Optimize** | Refinement | Give improvement instructions. Tell the AI what to fix, tighten, expand, simplify, or reorganize. |
| **P, Prompt again** | Iteration | Run the next version. Use a follow-up prompt so the answer improves step by step. |

## Why this template works

Many beginners treat prompting like a vending machine. Insert prompt, receive perfect result. Sadly, AI does not always work like that. Sometimes it is more like microwaving leftovers. You usually need to check, stir, and heat again.

LOOP works because it gives you a repeatable improvement process:

1. **Look** at the result as a whole.
2. **Observe** the details.
3. **Optimize** with targeted feedback.
4. **Prompt again** to get a stronger version.

This matters because most bad outputs are not total failures. They are often close, but not quite right. LOOP helps you fix near-misses instead of starting over from scratch every time.

It also helps you become a better judge of AI output. That is a big deal. Prompting is not only about asking well. It is also about reviewing well.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> **LOOK:** Evaluate the draft email below for clarity, professionalism, and conciseness. Give it a score from 1 to 10.  
> **OBSERVE:** Identify at least 3 specific issues, including awkward wording, missing details, or unnecessary length.  
> **OPTIMIZE:** Rewrite the email to sound confident, clear, and polite, while keeping it under 150 words.  
> **PROMPT AGAIN:** After rewriting, explain what you changed and suggest one additional follow-up version for a more formal audience.  
>   
> Draft email:  
> "Hi team, I just wanted to reach out and say that maybe we should think about moving the meeting because some people might not be able to make it and I think it could be better for everyone if we did Thursday instead maybe around 2."

**How the template shows up:**
- **L, Look:** The prompt asks the AI to evaluate the email for clarity, professionalism, and conciseness, and score it.
- **O, Observe:** It asks for at least 3 specific issues in the draft.
- **O, Optimize:** It tells the AI to rewrite the email with clearer goals and a word limit.
- **P, Prompt again:** It asks for a follow-up layer, explanation of changes, and an additional version for a different audience.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt is strong because it does not just ask, "Make this better." It defines exactly how to review the draft, what problems to look for, and what kind of improved output is needed. The final step keeps the loop going by asking for a refined version plus one more option. That produces a better result than vague editing requests.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> I wrote a short product description, but I think it sounds bland.  
>   
> - Look at it like a marketing editor and rate it from 1 to 10 for clarity and persuasiveness.  
> - Observe what is weak, especially boring phrases, missing benefits, or anything too generic.  
> - Optimize it so it sounds more engaging and customer-focused, but keep it under 80 words.  
> - Prompt again with 2 revised options: one professional, one more upbeat.  
>   
> Product description:  
> "This water bottle is durable and easy to use. It holds a lot of water and is good for daily activities."

**How the template shows up:**
- **L, Look:** The AI is asked to review the description and score clarity and persuasiveness.
- **O, Observe:** The prompt requests specific weak points, such as generic wording and missing benefits.
- **O, Optimize:** It asks for a stronger rewrite with a clear style goal and length limit.
- **P, Prompt again:** It requests 2 revised options in different tones, which extends the improvement cycle.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version feels lighter and more natural than the formal format, but it still follows the LOOP structure. It guides the AI through analysis before rewriting, which usually leads to sharper results. Asking for two rewritten options also makes comparison easier. That is useful because sometimes the best way to improve an AI response is to see alternatives side by side.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> I am working on a short LinkedIn post about finishing a big team project. First, tell me what is weak about this draft and what a reader might find unclear or forgettable. Then rewrite it so it sounds confident, specific, and professional without sounding stiff. Keep it under 120 words, and after that give me one stronger second version with a better opening line. Here is the draft: "Proud to share that our team completed an important project. It was a great experience and I learned a lot. Thanks to everyone involved."

**How the template shows up:**
- **L, Look:** "Tell me what is weak about this draft" asks for an overall review.
- **O, Observe:** "What a reader might find unclear or forgettable" pushes the AI to identify specific issues.
- **O, Optimize:** "Rewrite it so it sounds confident, specific, and professional" gives clear refinement instructions.
- **P, Prompt again:** "Give me one stronger second version with a better opening line" continues the iteration.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt sounds like normal conversation, but it still uses LOOP. That is the beauty of it. You do not always need tidy labels to prompt well. The structure helps the AI critique, improve, and retry in one flow. It is especially useful for writing tasks where the first draft is often acceptable, but not memorable.

## Warnings and extra tips

LOOP is powerful, but a few things can still go sideways:

- **Do not skip the evaluation step.** If you jump straight to "make it better," the AI may improve the wrong thing.
- **Be specific when you observe problems.** "This is bad" is not useful. "Too vague, too long, and missing examples" is much better.
- **Do not over-loop simple tasks.** If you only need a quick dinner recipe, you probably do not need a full quality review process.
- **Watch for false confidence.** The AI may sound sure of itself while still being wrong. For factual work, verify important claims.
- **Set realistic expectations.** LOOP improves output, but it does not guarantee perfection. Sometimes the source material is weak, incomplete, or confused to begin with.
- **Add criteria when needed.** If quality matters, tell the AI what "good" means. For example: accurate, concise, persuasive, beginner-friendly, or legally cautious.
- **Use follow-up prompts freely.** LOOP is not always a one-pass template. You can repeat the cycle again if the new version still needs work.

A useful habit is to ask the AI to score its own response, then explain the score. That alone can reveal weak spots you would not have noticed at first glance.

## Quick summary

LOOP stands for **Look, Observe, Optimize, Prompt again**. It teaches one of the most important prompt engineering habits: **good prompting is iterative**. Instead of expecting the first answer to be perfect, review it, spot the issues, improve the instructions, and run another round.

Try LOOP on something you already wrote, like an email, social post, resume bullet, or product description. Start small. Ask the AI to review it, point out what is weak, improve it, and give you a second version. That is where prompting starts to feel less like guessing and more like actually steering the machine.
