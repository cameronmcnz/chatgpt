# DRIFT

DRIFT is a simple way to remember that your first prompt is usually not your final prompt. You **define** what you want, **refine** the direction, **iterate** with another pass, ask for **feedback**, and **tune** the result until it actually works.

In other words, DRIFT is what you use when your first AI answer is "close enough to be annoying."

## What the template means

| Letter or word | Prompt ingredient | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| D, Define | Task | States what you want the AI to do clearly and specifically. |
| R, Refine | Refinement | Adds improvements, constraints, or clearer direction after the first attempt. |
| I, Iterate | Iteration | Continues the process with follow-up prompts instead of stopping too early. |
| F, Feedback | Feedback | Tells the AI what is working, what is missing, and what should change. |
| T, Tune | Refinement | Polishes the final result for tone, structure, length, clarity, or usefulness. |

## Why this template works

DRIFT works because prompt writing is not a one-shot activity. Most weak prompts fail for one of two reasons:

1. The task is too vague.
2. The user expects the first answer to be perfect.

DRIFT fixes both problems.

First, it helps you **start with a defined task** instead of tossing vague wishes into the chatbot and hoping for magic. Then it reminds you that good prompting is a loop. You improve the response by guiding it, reacting to it, and tightening it.

This is especially useful for writing, planning, summaries, emails, lesson materials, research drafts, and anything else where "pretty good" is not the finish line.

Think of DRIFT like steering a car, not launching a paper airplane. Small adjustments matter.

## 3 sample prompts

### Sample prompt 1: Formal version

> DEFINE: Draft a professional email to a client explaining that our project deadline must move back by one week due to unexpected testing delays.  
> REFINE: Keep the tone confident, accountable, and reassuring. Do not sound defensive.  
> ITERATE: After writing the first version, provide two improved alternatives, one more formal and one more warm.  
> FEEDBACK: Briefly explain the strengths and weaknesses of each version, especially clarity and tone.  
> TUNE: Recommend the best final version and polish it to under 180 words.

**How the template shows up:**
- **D, Define:** The task is clear, write a client email about a delayed deadline.
- **R, Refine:** The tone is specified as confident, accountable, and reassuring.
- **I, Iterate:** The AI is asked to create multiple versions after the first draft.
- **F, Feedback:** The AI must review each version and explain strengths and weaknesses.
- **T, Tune:** The final output is polished and trimmed to under 180 words.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This prompt does not just ask for an email. It asks for a draft, a comparison, feedback, and a polished final version. That leads to a stronger result because the AI is not guessing what "better" means. The prompt builds improvement directly into the workflow. Instead of writing one email and hoping it lands, you get options, analysis, and a final tuned version.

### Sample prompt 2: Semi-structured version

> Help me improve a workshop title and description for a beginner AI class.  
>  
> Start with 5 title options and a 100-word class description.  
> Then refine them so they sound practical, friendly, and not hypey.  
> After that, suggest what is weak or unclear about your first draft.  
> Revise the best version based on that feedback.  
> Finally, tune it for a professional audience at a local business association.

**How the template shows up:**
- **D, Define:** The core task is to create a workshop title and description for a beginner AI class.
- **R, Refine:** The prompt asks for a practical, friendly tone and explicitly says not hypey.
- **I, Iterate:** It requests an initial draft, then a revision, then a best version.
- **F, Feedback:** The AI is asked to point out what is weak or unclear in its own first draft.
- **T, Tune:** The final version is adjusted for a specific audience, a local business association.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
This version feels more natural than the formal template, but it still follows the DRIFT logic. The user gets multiple title ideas, a draft description, a critique, and a stronger revision. That is far better than asking, "Write me a workshop blurb," then wondering why it sounds like a robot trying to sell vitamins on late-night TV.

### Sample prompt 3: Natural version

> I need a short LinkedIn post about using AI to save time on meeting notes. Give me a first version, then tell me what feels too generic, rewrite it to sound more specific and credible, and finish with a polished version that sounds like an experienced manager wrote it.

**How the template shows up:**
- **D, Define:** The prompt clearly asks for a short LinkedIn post about AI and meeting notes.
- **R, Refine:** It asks for the post to become more specific and credible.
- **I, Iterate:** The AI is told to give a first version, then rewrite it, then finish with a polished version.
- **F, Feedback:** The AI must identify what feels too generic in the first version.
- **T, Tune:** The final version is tuned to sound like it came from an experienced manager.

**Why it improves the prompt:**
Even though this sounds casual, it still follows a strong improvement loop. The user is not asking for perfection on the first try. They are asking for a draft, a critique, a rewrite, and a polished final version. That sequence usually produces content that sounds sharper, more human, and more useful.

## Warnings and extra tips

DRIFT is powerful, but it is not magic. A few things to watch out for:

- **Do not skip Define.** If the task is fuzzy, the rest of the loop is just polishing confusion.
- **Do not over-iterate.** At some point, more revision just creates word salad with better posture.
- **Ask for specific feedback.** "Make it better" is weak. "Make it clearer for first-time managers" is useful.
- **Tune for a real audience.** Final polish works best when you specify who will read it.
- **Do not expect the AI to judge perfectly on its own.** Its feedback can be helpful, but your judgment still matters.
- **Use DRIFT when quality matters.** For a quick fact lookup, this may be overkill. You do not need a five-step improvement loop to ask for pancake recipes.
- **Bring your own standards.** If you care about tone, accuracy, compliance, or brand style, say so. The AI cannot read your mind, which is honestly rude of it.

One more tip: DRIFT works especially well when you paste in an existing draft and ask the AI to improve it step by step. That gives the model something concrete to work with, which usually leads to better results than starting from nothing.

## Quick summary

DRIFT stands for **Define, Refine, Iterate, Feedback, Tune**. It helps you stop treating prompting like a single command and start treating it like a smart editing process.

If your first result is decent but not great, do not start over blindly. DRIFT it.

Try this next: take an email, social post, summary, or work document you already have, and run it through the five DRIFT steps. You will quickly see that better prompts are often just better follow-up.
