# Day 2: Foundational Prompt Engineering Techniques

Yesterday, you met ChatGPT. Today, you learn how to stop treating it like a search box with delusions of grandeur.

Day 2 is all about building practical, repeatable prompt patterns that help you get clearer, more useful, and more reliable responses. You do not need to become a programmer. You do not need to memorize a secret language. You just need a few simple structures that help you explain what you want, what you do not want, and what a good answer should look like.

Think of this as moving from “Hey, can you help me with this?” to “Here is the task, here is the audience, here is the tone, here are the constraints, and here is the format I need.” That small shift makes a very big difference.

## What this day is about

Today focuses on foundational prompt engineering techniques. That means you will learn beginner-friendly prompt patterns you can use again and again.

Instead of staring at a blank prompt box and hoping inspiration shows up wearing a cape, you will have frameworks to guide you. These frameworks help you include the details ChatGPT needs to produce better work.

We will look at prompt structures like MEOW, CRAP, PAWS, TREAT, and POWER. Yes, some of the names are a little ridiculous. That is partly the point. They are memorable, and if a mildly silly template helps you write better prompts, we are not going to argue with success.

You will also learn how to use ChatGPT in different roles, such as tutor, editor, reviewer, and judge. This matters because ChatGPT can do more than answer questions. It can help you think, revise, compare, explain, improve, and pressure-test your work.

## Why this matters

Most disappointing ChatGPT answers come from vague prompts.

If you ask a vague question, you usually get a vague answer. ChatGPT may still sound confident, which is both impressive and mildly annoying, but confidence is not the same as usefulness.

For example, a prompt like “Write an email” leaves too much open:

- Who is the email for?
- What is the goal?
- Should it be formal, friendly, brief, persuasive, apologetic, or direct?
- What details must be included?
- What should be avoided?
- What format do you need?

Prompt engineering solves this problem by helping you give ChatGPT better instructions. You are not trying to “trick” the AI. You are learning how to communicate clearly with it.

This day matters because it gives you a reusable process. Once you learn these patterns, you can apply them to writing, planning, learning, editing, research, brainstorming, decision-making, and everyday work tasks.

Basically, you are learning how to stop getting “technically correct but not what I wanted” answers. A noble cause.

## What you will learn

By the end of this day, you will be able to build prompts that are more specific, more useful, and easier for ChatGPT to follow.

You will learn how to:

- Use simple frameworks like **MEOW**, **CRAP**, **PAWS**, **TREAT**, and **POWER** to structure your prompts.
- Add the key ingredients that improve results, including context, constraints, audience, tone, and output format.
- Ask ChatGPT to improve a weak prompt before you use it.
- Ask ChatGPT to ask you clarifying questions when your request is not specific enough.
- Use examples to show ChatGPT what kind of answer you want.
- Give step-by-step instructions so ChatGPT follows a process instead of guessing.
- Ask ChatGPT to act as a tutor, editor, reviewer, or judge depending on the task.

The main skill you are building is control. Not total control, because this is AI, not a microwave from 1993 with three buttons and a questionable popcorn setting. But you will gain much better control over the quality, structure, and usefulness of the responses you receive.

## What we will cover

We will start with practical prompt frameworks.

**MEOW** helps you think through the mission, examples, output, and warnings. It is a friendly structure for telling ChatGPT what to do and what to avoid.

**CRAP** focuses on context, restrictions, aim, and persona. The name may be unfortunate, but the framework is useful. It helps you keep prompts grounded and specific.

**PAWS**, **TREAT**, and **POWER** give you additional ways to organize your instructions. Each framework emphasizes slightly different pieces, such as role, audience, tone, structure, examples, and objective.

You will also learn how to make ChatGPT part of the prompting process. Instead of trying to write the perfect prompt on your own, you can ask ChatGPT to improve it. You can also ask it to interview you first by asking clarifying questions.

Then we will look at role-based prompting, often called “act as” prompting. This is where you ask ChatGPT to respond as a tutor, editor, judge, reviewer, coach, strategist, or another useful role.

Finally, we will introduce examples and step-by-step instructions. You will see how showing ChatGPT a sample can guide the style and format of the answer. You will also learn why breaking a task into steps often produces better results than tossing one giant request into the chat and hoping for the best.

## Where this becomes useful

These skills show up everywhere.

You can use them when you need to:

- Write clearer emails, reports, proposals, or messages.
- Turn messy notes into organized summaries.
- Ask ChatGPT to explain a confusing topic like a patient tutor.
- Get feedback on something you wrote before sending it.
- Compare options and ask ChatGPT to judge them against specific criteria.
- Create outlines, checklists, lesson plans, meeting agendas, or project plans.
- Rewrite content for a specific audience, such as customers, coworkers, managers, students, or clients.
- Improve a weak prompt instead of starting over from scratch.
- Get ChatGPT to ask better questions before giving an answer.

This is where ChatGPT starts to feel less like a novelty and more like a practical assistant. The quality of the conversation improves because you know how to guide it.

## Quick summary

Day 2 gives you the core prompt engineering patterns you will use throughout the rest of the course. You will learn how to add context, set boundaries, define the audience, request a format, provide examples, and assign useful roles to ChatGPT.

The goal is simple: better prompts, better answers.

Once you understand these foundational techniques, you will no longer have to rely on guesswork. You will have a set of repeatable tools that make ChatGPT more useful for real tasks, not just impressive party tricks.
