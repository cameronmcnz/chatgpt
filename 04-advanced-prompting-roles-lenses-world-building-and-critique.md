# Day 4: Advanced Prompting: Roles, Lenses, World Building, and Critique

By now, you have learned that ChatGPT is not just a search box with better manners. It can explain, summarize, brainstorm, organize, rewrite, and help you think through problems.

Today, we take a big step forward.

Instead of simply asking ChatGPT a question and hoping the answer is useful, you will learn how to design the interaction. You will guide the assistant by giving it a role, a point of view, an audience, a tone, a scenario, constraints, and a process for improving the answer.

In other words, you stop being a person tossing questions into the void and start becoming the director of the conversation. Clipboard optional. Dramatic director’s chair also optional, but encouraged.

## What this day is about

Day 4 is about moving from basic prompting to richer, more intentional prompting.

A basic prompt might be:

> Explain this idea.

A more advanced prompt might be:

> Explain this idea as a patient teacher speaking to a beginner. Use simple language, give one example, avoid jargon, and then ask me two questions to check my understanding.

That second prompt gives ChatGPT a job, an audience, a tone, and a structure. The result is usually much more useful because the model has a clearer target.

Today, you will learn how to shape ChatGPT’s responses using:

- Roles, such as teacher, editor, strategist, coach, developer, or critic
- Lenses, such as beginner-friendly, executive-level, customer-focused, or technical
- Audience and tone choices, so the answer fits the reader
- Constraints, so the response stays focused instead of wandering off like a meeting with no agenda
- Scenarios and world-building, so you can create richer creative or training situations
- Debate, critique, and red team prompts, so ChatGPT can help test ideas instead of just agreeing politely

The goal is not to make prompts longer for the sake of it. Nobody needs a 900-word prompt to ask for a grocery list. The goal is to make your prompts more intentional when the task actually matters.

## Why this matters

One of the biggest beginner frustrations with ChatGPT is inconsistency.

Sometimes the answer is great. Sometimes it is vague. Sometimes it is too formal, too long, too shallow, too technical, too cheerful, or weirdly confident about something it should probably be less confident about.

Advanced prompting helps you fix that.

When you know how to assign roles, apply lenses, and use constraints, you can get answers that are better matched to your actual situation. You are no longer limited to “tell me about this.” You can ask ChatGPT to think like a hiring manager, explain like a tutor, review like a skeptical editor, plan like a project manager, or critique like someone who is not afraid to hurt your feelings a little.

That last one is useful. Painful, maybe. But useful.

This day also matters because ChatGPT is naturally cooperative. That is usually helpful, but it can also mean it agrees too quickly. If you are making a decision, planning a project, writing a proposal, building a product, or testing an idea, you do not just want applause. You want pressure testing.

Today teaches you how to ask for that.

## What you will learn

By the end of this day, you will be able to guide ChatGPT in a much more deliberate way.

You will learn how to:

- **Use role prompting** to change the perspective of the answer. You can ask ChatGPT to respond as a coach, editor, teacher, analyst, customer, or subject matter expert.
- **Apply different lenses** depending on the situation. A beginner lens creates a different answer than an executive lens, and a critic lens creates a very different answer than a cheerleader lens.
- **Shape answers for specific audiences** so your output fits the people who will actually read or use it.
- **Control tone and style** without getting stuck with generic “professional but friendly” writing forever.
- **Add constraints** that keep the response focused, practical, brief, detailed, structured, or creative.
- **Build scenarios and worlds** with rules, characters, settings, and limitations, especially useful for training, roleplay, writing, simulations, and creative projects.
- **Use archetypes and story structures** like the hero’s journey to create more engaging content and ideas.
- **Ask ChatGPT to debate, challenge, or red team an idea** so you can find weaknesses before someone else does.
- **Run critique and revision loops** to improve an answer over multiple passes instead of accepting the first draft like it came down from a mountain carved in stone.

The big idea: great prompting is not just about getting an answer. It is about creating a useful thinking process.

## What we will cover

We will start with **role prompting**, where you give ChatGPT a clear identity or function for the task. This can be as simple as “act as a writing coach” or as specific as “act as a skeptical CFO reviewing this budget proposal.”

Then we will look at **lens prompting**, which is about choosing the perspective ChatGPT uses. The same topic can be explained through the lens of a beginner, a customer, a teacher, an executive, a developer, or a critic. Each lens highlights different details.

From there, we will work with **audience prompting** and **tone prompting**, because a message for a new employee should not sound the same as a message for a board meeting, a customer email, or a social media post. Context matters. Tone matters. Accidentally sounding like a corporate robot from 1997 also matters.

Next, we will use **constraint prompting** to set boundaries. Constraints might include length, format, reading level, examples, steps, exclusions, or style rules. Good constraints help ChatGPT produce something usable instead of something you have to wrestle into shape afterward.

We will also explore **scenario prompting** and **world-building prompts**, which are especially powerful for simulations, creative writing, training exercises, games, learning environments, and practice conversations. You will see how rules, settings, characters, and stakes can make an interaction more realistic and useful.

Finally, we will get into **debate prompting**, **red team prompting**, and **critique and revision loops**. This is where ChatGPT becomes more than a helper. It becomes a sparring partner, reviewer, challenger, and editor.

## Where this becomes useful

You will use today’s skills whenever you need a better answer than “Here are five generic tips.”

For example, you can use these techniques to:

- Rewrite an email for a specific audience and tone
- Practice a difficult conversation before having it
- Ask ChatGPT to review a plan from a customer’s point of view
- Turn a rough idea into a structured proposal
- Create training scenarios for employees or students
- Brainstorm story ideas, characters, settings, and conflicts
- Ask for a skeptical critique of a business idea
- Improve a first draft through multiple revision passes
- Prepare for questions, objections, or pushback
- Simplify a complicated topic for someone new to it

These are practical skills. They help you get clearer writing, sharper thinking, stronger plans, and more useful feedback.

You will learn how to give ChatGPT roles, lenses, audiences, tones, constraints, scenarios, and critique tasks. You will also learn how to push beyond the first answer and improve results through revision loops.

The payoff is simple: better prompts, better responses, better thinking.
